Paris Landmarks: A Brief Reference

The Eiffel Tower
The Eiffel Tower was completed in 1889 for the Exposition Universelle (World's Fair) held to celebrate the 100th anniversary of the French Revolution. It was designed by the engineer Gustave Eiffel and his team, and it stands 330 meters tall including its broadcast antennas. At the time of its completion it was the tallest man-made structure in the world, a title it held for 41 years until the Chrysler Building was finished in New York in 1930. The tower was originally intended as a temporary structure and was scheduled to be dismantled after 20 years, but it was preserved because of its value as a radiotelegraph station.

Notre-Dame Cathedral
Construction of Notre-Dame de Paris began in 1163 under Bishop Maurice de Sully and was largely completed in 1345. It is one of the finest examples of French Gothic architecture and is famous for its rib vaults, flying buttresses, and rose windows. A devastating fire in April 2019 destroyed the spire and most of the wooden roof; restoration work has been ongoing since.

The Louvre
The Louvre was originally built as a fortress in the late 12th century, but it became a royal palace and was eventually opened to the public as a museum in 1793, during the French Revolution. It is the world's most-visited museum and houses approximately 38,000 objects, including the Mona Lisa, the Venus de Milo, and the Winged Victory of Samothrace. The famous glass pyramid in the main courtyard was designed by I. M. Pei and inaugurated in 1989.

The Arc de Triomphe
The Arc de Triomphe stands at the western end of the Champs-Élysées and was completed in 1836. It was commissioned by Napoleon in 1806 to honor those who fought and died for France during the French Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars. Beneath the arch lies the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier from World War I, with an eternal flame that has been rekindled every evening since 1923.

Sacré-Cœur Basilica
The Basilica of the Sacred Heart of Paris, commonly known as Sacré-Cœur, sits at the summit of the butte Montmartre, the highest point in the city. Construction began in 1875 and was completed in 1914, but the basilica was not formally consecrated until 1919, after the end of World War I. It is built of travertine stone that exudes calcite over time, ensuring that the building remains white even with weathering and pollution.

The Panthéon
The Panthéon in the Latin Quarter was originally built as a church dedicated to Saint Geneviève but became a mausoleum during the French Revolution. It was completed in 1790. It is the resting place of distinguished French citizens including Voltaire, Rousseau, Victor Hugo, Marie Curie, and Alexandre Dumas. The famous Foucault pendulum was first publicly demonstrated there in 1851.
